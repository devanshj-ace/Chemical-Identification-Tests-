A — Tollens' Reagent Test
Core Theory & Reaction ★★★★★
Reagent: AgNO₃ + excess NH₄OH → [Ag(NH₃)₂]⁺OH⁻
// ammoniacal silver nitrate · must be freshly prepared — explosive Ag₃N forms on storage
RCHO + 2[Ag(NH₃)₂]⁺ + 2OH⁻ → RCOO⁻ + 2Ag↓ (mirror) + 4NH₃ + H₂O
// Ag⁺ oxidizes aldehyde → acid; Ag⁺ reduced to Ag metal = silver mirror
  • ✓ Positive (gives silver mirror): All aliphatic aldehydes (HCHO, CH₃CHO, RCHO), all aromatic aldehydes (PhCHO, p-NO₂C₆H₄CHO), formic acid (HCOOH), glucose, fructose, maltose, lactose, glycolaldehyde
  • ✗ Negative: All ketones (except α-hydroxy ketones in some cases), sucrose, starch, cellulose, all alcohols, acetic acid, propionic acid, all other carboxylic acids except HCOOH
  • Why HCOOH is positive: Structure H–C(=O)–OH has a free –CHO group; unique among carboxylic acids
  • Why ketones fail: No α-H on the carbonyl carbon available for oxidation; Cu²⁺/Ag⁺ cannot oxidize the C–C bond easily
  • Reaction type: Oxidation-reduction (aldehyde oxidized to carboxylate; Ag⁺ reduced to Ag⁰)
⚠
JEE Trap #1: Terminal alkynes (RC≡CH) give a WHITE PRECIPITATE of silver acetylide (RC≡CAg), NOT a silver mirror. This tests C–H acidity (pKa ~25), not reducing ability. The mirror test = aldehyde only.
JEE Trap #2: Fructose gives +ve Tollens' despite being a ketone — tautomerism in alkaline medium (enediol → glucose).
Special Cases & JEE-High-Yield Facts
  • ⭐ Acetaldehyde (CH₃CHO): +ve Tollens', +ve Fehling's, +ve Iodoform — the ONLY compound with all three. Tested repeatedly in JEE.
  • ⭐ Formaldehyde (HCHO): +ve Tollens', +ve Fehling's, but NEGATIVE iodoform (no CH₃CO– group)
  • ⭐ Fructose: +ve Tollens' & Fehling's via Lobry de Bruyn–van Ekenstein transformation (enediol tautomerism in base)
  • ⭐ Sucrose: NEGATIVE — glycosidic bond locks C-1 (glucose) and C-2 (fructose) anomeric carbons — non-reducing sugar
  • Maltose & Lactose: +ve Tollens' — reducing disaccharides with free –CHO group
  • Glyoxal (OHC–CHO): +ve Tollens' — has two –CHO groups
  • Benzoin (PhCH(OH)COPh): α-hydroxy ketone → can give +ve Tollens' via enolization in base
  • Tollens' does NOT distinguish HCHO from RCHO — both give mirror; use iodoform for HCHO (negative) vs CH₃CHO (positive)
💡Sensitivity: Tollens' is very sensitive — works at room temperature. Warming the test tube speeds up the reaction and gives a brighter mirror.
B — Fehling's Solution & Benedict's Test
Fehling's Solution — Composition & Reaction ★★★★
Fehling A: CuSO₄(aq)  |  Fehling B: Sodium potassium tartrate (Rochelle salt) + NaOH
// mixed 1:1 just before use · tartrate complexes Cu²⁺ to prevent Cu(OH)₂ precipitation · Cu²⁺ + tartrate = deep blue complex
RCHO + 2Cu²⁺(tartrate complex) + 5OH⁻ → RCOO⁻ + Cu₂O↓ (brick-red) + 3H₂O
// Cu²⁺ → Cu⁺ (Cu₂O) · brick-red precipitate = positive · solution changes from blue → green → brick-red
  • ✓ Positive: All aliphatic aldehydes (HCHO, CH₃CHO etc.), formic acid (HCOOH), glucose, fructose, maltose, lactose, glyceraldehyde, dihydroxyacetone (α-hydroxy ketone)
  • ✗ Negative: Benzaldehyde (PhCHO) and ALL aromatic aldehydes, all ketones (except α-hydroxy ketones in alkaline), sucrose, starch, alcohols
  • ⭐ Key JEE distinction: Fehling's SEPARATES aliphatic from aromatic aldehydes. PhCHO gives +ve Tollens' but –ve Fehling's.
  • Why aromatic aldehydes fail: Aromatic ring delocalizes electron density → –CHO is less electron-rich/reactive → cannot reduce the Cu²⁺ complex
  • Why fructose passes: In hot alkaline Fehling's, fructose tautomerizes (enediol form) → partially converts to glucose → reduces Cu²⁺
⚠
Trap: Fehling's test must be heated (hot) — unlike Tollens' which works at room temperature. Cold Fehling's may not give a visible result even with aliphatic aldehydes.
Benedict's Test & Comparison ★★★
Reagent: CuSO₄ + Sodium citrate + Na₂CO₃ in one stable solution
// citrate is a better stabilizer than tartrate · single-bottle reagent · more stable on storage
  • Same positive/negative list as Fehling's — identical chemistry, same compounds pass/fail
  • Benedict's is more sensitive than Fehling's — can detect lower concentrations of glucose
  • Benedict's is a single stable solution; Fehling's requires mixing A and B fresh each time
  • Clinical standard for detecting glucose in urine (semi-quantitative — colour gradient from blue→green→yellow→orange→red indicates glucose concentration)
  • Benedict's gives colour gradient: Blue (0 g/dL) → Green → Yellow → Orange → Brick-red (>2 g/dL)
  • Cannot distinguish reducing sugars from each other — only tells if reducing sugar is present
  • Starch, cellulose, sucrose, trehalose (all non-reducing) → NEGATIVE both tests
💡JEE Point: For JEE purposes, Benedict's and Fehling's are interchangeable in results. Just remember: both fail with aromatic aldehydes and pure ketones. Benedict's is more clinically used.
★Shortcut: If a compound gives +ve Fehling's, it MUST also give +ve Tollens'. The reverse is NOT true (PhCHO = +ve Tollens', –ve Fehling's).
C — Haloform Reaction & Iodoform Test
Reagent, Reaction & Mechanism ★★★★★
Reagent (Iodoform): I₂ + NaOH(aq) or NaI + NaOCl · warm
General Haloform: X₂ + NaOH (X = Cl, Br, I)
// yellow CHI₃ ppt with antiseptic smell = +ve · CHCl₃ (chloroform) & CHBr₃ (bromoform) also form with Cl₂/Br₂
CH₃CO–R + 3I₂ + 3NaOH → CI₃CO–R + 3NaI + 3H₂O (step 1–3)
CI₃CO–R + NaOH → CHI₃↓ (yellow) + RCOONa (step 4)
⚠ Must Remember — Mechanism (JEE-Level, 4 Steps)
1
α-Deprotonation: NaOH removes α-H from CH₃ group → carbanion forms
2
Halogenation × 3: Carbanion attacks I₂ → α-C substituted 3 times → CI₃CO–R (triiodo intermediate)
3
Nucleophilic addition: OH⁻ attacks the carbonyl carbon of CI₃CO–R → tetrahedral intermediate
4
Elimination: CI₃⁻ is an excellent leaving group → CHI₃ (iodoform) + RCOONa formed
Positive & Negative Compounds — Complete List
  • ⭐ Structural requirement: CH₃CO–R (methyl ketone) OR CH₃CH(OH)–R (methyl secondary carbinol)
  • ✓ Methyl ketones (CH₃COR): Acetone (CH₃COCH₃), MEK (CH₃COC₂H₅), methyl n-propyl ketone, methyl i-propyl ketone
  • ✓ Only aldehyde: Acetaldehyde (CH₃CHO) — has CH₃CO– group
  • ✓ Acetophenone (PhCOCH₃) ⭐: Has CH₃CO– group → positive (very frequently tested!)
  • ✓ Methyl carbinols CH₃CH(OH)R: Ethanol (C₂H₅OH), isopropanol ((CH₃)₂CHOH), 2-butanol, 2-pentanol, butan-2-ol
  • ✗ HCHO: No CH₃CO– group — NEGATIVE (most common trap)
  • ✗ Higher aldehydes (propanal, butanal, PhCHO): No CH₃CO– group → NEGATIVE
  • ✗ Methanol (CH₃OH): NaOH/I₂ oxidizes → HCHO (no CH₃CO–) → NEGATIVE
  • ✗ 1-propanol, n-butanol, 1-pentanol (primary alcohols except ethanol): Give non-methyl aldehydes/ketones → NEGATIVE
  • ✗ Diethyl ketone (CH₃CH₂COCH₂CH₃): No CH₃CO– group → NEGATIVE
  • ⭐ Ethanol → iodoform pathway: NaOH/I₂ first oxidizes EtOH to CH₃CHO, then iodoform reaction proceeds
⚠
Trick question: "How many of these give iodoform: acetone, HCHO, iPrOH, PhCOCH₃, n-butanol, 2-butanol?"
Answer: 4 — acetone ✓, iPrOH ✓, PhCOCH₃ ✓, 2-butanol ✓. HCHO ✗ (no CH₃CO–), n-butanol ✗ (→ butanal).
D — Victor Meyer Test
Purpose, Reagents & Steps ★★★★
  • Purpose: Classify 1°, 2°, 3° alcohols using colour observation — one of the most reliable tests
  • Reagents: (i) Red P + I₂ (ii) AgNO₂ (iii) HNO₂ (generated from NaNO₂ + dil. HCl) (iv) KOH
  • Basis: Different structural response of primary, secondary and tertiary nitroalkanes to nitrous acid (HNO₂) — tautomerism is key
1
Alcohol → Alkyl Iodide: R–OH + Red P + I₂ → R–I + H₂O (or use PCl₅/SOCl₂)
2
Alkyl Iodide → Nitroalkane: R–I + AgNO₂ → R–NO₂ + AgI↓ (silver nitrite = source of NO₂⁻)
3
Nitroalkane + HNO₂: Add NaNO₂ + dil. HCl → HNO₂ in situ → react with nitroalkane
4
Add KOH/NaOH base → observe final stable colour
⚠
Confusion with Hinsberg's test: Hinsberg's (with benzenesulfonyl chloride) classifies 1°/2°/3° AMINES. Victor Meyer classifies 1°/2°/3° ALCOHOLS. Don't mix them up!

★JEE Extra: The 1° red colour ONLY appears in alkaline (KOH) medium — it is the potassium nitrolic acid salt that is red. Acidify it and it turns colourless. This acid-base colour change is a secondary observation often tested.

JEE Mains & Advanced Trends 2020–2026 ★★★★★
  • ⭐ [Most tested, 2020–2025]: "Which compound gives +ve iodoform but –ve Tollens'?" → Acetone, MEK, PhCOCH₃, iPrOH — methyl ketones/carbinols that aren't aldehydes
  • ⭐ [JEE Mains ~2021, Assertion-Reason]: A: Fructose gives Tollens' test. R: Fructose contains a free aldehyde group. → A is TRUE, R is FALSE (correct reason = tautomerism). Very high yield.

  • [JEE Advanced ~2022, Matrix Match]: Formic acid, benzaldehyde, acetophenone, glucose matched to test results — requires knowing PhCHO fails Fehling's; HCOOH passes Tollens'
  • [Post-2023 shift]: More application-based — "Product X formed from reaction Y; does X give Tollens' test?" Combines mechanism + test knowledge
  • [2024–25 trend]: Carbohydrate-heavy: reducing vs non-reducing sugars, why fructose behaves like aldehyde, glycosidic bond and its role in non-reducing character of sucrose
  • Question types seen: Integer (count), Multiple Correct, Match the Following, Assertion-Reason, Comprehension (sequential identification)
Common Student Mistakes & Golden Facts
⚠ 8 Most Common Mistakes in JEE
  • ✗ Mistake 1: Thinking PhCHO gives Fehling's. It does NOT — aromatic aldehydes fail Fehling's/Benedict's.
  • ✗ Mistake 2: Thinking HCHO gives iodoform. It does NOT — no CH₃CO– group adjacent to carbonyl.
  • ✗ Mistake 3: Terminal alkyne → "silver mirror." WRONG — gives white silver acetylide precipitate, not mirror.
  • ✗ Mistake 4: Sucrose gives +ve Fehling's because it contains glucose+fructose. WRONG — sucrose is non-reducing; the glycosidic bond prevents reaction.
  • ✗ Mistake 5: Methanol gives iodoform. WRONG — NaOH/I₂ → HCHO (not CH₃CHO), so no iodoform.
  • ✗ Mistake 6: Acetophenone (PhCOCH₃) gives –ve iodoform. WRONG — PhCOCH₃ has CH₃CO– → +ve iodoform ⭐
  • ✗ Mistake 7: All ketones fail Tollens'. WRONG — α-hydroxy ketones (e.g., fructose, benzoin) can pass via enolization.
  • ✗ Mistake 8: Victor Meyer tests amines. WRONG — Victor Meyer = alcohols; Hinsberg's = amines.

G — Cannizzaro Reaction
Core Theory, Mechanism & Kinetics ★★★★★
  • Definition: Aldehydes with NO α-hydrogen undergo disproportionation in conc. NaOH → one molecule oxidized (→ carboxylate), one reduced (→ alcohol)
  • Substrates: HCHO, PhCHO, trimethylacetaldehyde (pivaldehyde), furfural, ClCH₂CHO — all have NO α-H
  • Does NOT occur with: CH₃CHO, CH₃CH₂CHO — these have α-H → undergo aldol condensation instead
  • Reagent: Concentrated NaOH (not dilute); aqueous medium
2 RCHO + NaOH(conc.) → RCH₂OH + RCOONa
// e.g. 2PhCHO + NaOH → PhCH₂OH (benzyl alcohol) + PhCOONa (sodium benzoate)
Mechanism — 3 Steps (JEE Advanced Level)
1
OH⁻ attacks C=O: Hydroxide attacks carbonyl of one RCHO → tetrahedral alkoxide intermediate R–CH(OH)–O⁻ (fast step)
2
Hydride (H⁻) transfer (RDS): Alkoxide donates H⁻ to carbonyl carbon of a SECOND RCHO molecule. Rate = k[RCHO]²[OH⁻]. This is the slow, rate-determining step.
3
Products form: One RCHO → RCOO⁻ (oxidized, loses H); other RCHO → RCH₂O⁻ → RCH₂OH (reduced, gains H⁻)
⚡Kinetics: Rate = k[RCHO]²[OH⁻]. 2nd order in aldehyde, 1st order in OH⁻. RDS = hydride transfer (step 2). Deuterium label: RCDO → D ends up in the alcohol product (RCH₂OH gets the D).
Special Cases, Cross-Cannizzaro & JEE Advanced Examples
  • ⭐ Intramolecular Cannizzaro: Glyoxal (OHC–CHO) + NaOH → glycolate (HOCH₂COO⁻) — one –CHO oxidized, other reduced within same molecule. Product = sodium glycolate.
  • ⭐ Cross-Cannizzaro (HCHO + RCHO + NaOH): HCHO is ALWAYS the reductant (best H⁻ donor) → HCHO oxidized to HCOONa; RCHO reduced to RCH₂OH. Never the other way around.
  • ⭐ EWG on ring (p-NO₂, p-Cl): More electrophilic C=O → faster OH⁻ addition (step 1) → faster overall rate
  • ⭐ EDG on ring (p-CH₃, p-OCH₃): Less electrophilic C=O → slower OH⁻ addition → slower rate
  • ⭐ Isotope labelling (¹⁸O): If RCHO has ¹⁸O at C=O → after Cannizzaro, ¹⁸O ends up in the ACID product (RCOO⁻). The OH⁻ oxygen goes into the alkoxide/alcohol. C=¹⁸O of oxidized molecule becomes C–¹⁸OO⁻.
  • ⭐ No-crossover between molecules: H⁻ transfer is intramolecular within the alkoxide-aldehyde complex — never a free hydride. Crossover experiments confirm this.
  • Phthalaldehyde (o-OHC–C₆H₄–CHO): Intramolecular Cannizzaro → o-hydroxymethylbenzoate
  • Aldol vs Cannizzaro selectivity: Aldehyde WITH α-H → always aldol in NaOH, never Cannizzaro. Mixed system: if one aldehyde has α-H, aldol dominates even if other has no α-H.
⚠
Critical JEE trap: In cross-Cannizzaro HCHO + PhCHO + NaOH → PhCH₂OH + HCOONa. HCHO is oxidized (→ formate), PhCHO is reduced (→ benzyl alcohol). Students often get this backwards.
H — Reimer–Tiemann Reaction
Core Theory, Mechanism & Salicylaldehyde ★★★★
  • Definition: Formylation of activated aromatic rings (especially phenols) using CHCl₃ + NaOH → introduces –CHO group ortho to –OH
  • Substrates: Phenol (main), resorcinol, β-naphthol. Benzene does NOT react — needs activation by –OH.
  • Reagent: CHCl₃ + aq. NaOH (conc.) at 60–70°C
C₆H₅OH + CHCl₃ + 3NaOH → o-HOC₆H₄CHO (salicylaldehyde) + 3NaCl + 2H₂O
// mainly ortho product; some para also formed · acidify to get free phenol-aldehyde
Mechanism — 4 Steps
1
Carbene generation (RDS): CHCl₃ + NaOH → CCl₃⁻ → :CCl₂ (dichlorocarbene) + Cl⁻ by α-elimination. :CCl₂ is the actual reactive electrophile.
2
Phenoxide formation: NaOH converts PhOH → PhO⁻ (phenoxide) — highly activated ring for electrophilic attack
3
Electrophilic attack: :CCl₂ attacks ortho (major) or para position of phenoxide → –CCl₂ adduct on ring
4
Hydrolysis: –CCl₂ adduct + NaOH/H₂O → –CHO + 2NaCl + H₂O → salicylaldehyde after acidification
★Salicylaldehyde → Salicylic acid: Reimer-Tiemann gives salicylaldehyde → oxidize with Ag₂O or KMnO₄ → salicylic acid. Compare: Kolbe-Schmitt (PhONa + CO₂, pressure) → salicylate directly. Both routes to salicylic acid but via different intermediates (aldehyde vs direct carboxylation).
Abnormal Reimer–Tiemann & Key Facts
  • ⭐ Normal R-T: Both ortho positions available → :CCl₂ attacks ortho C → –CHO introduced. Ortho major, para minor (due to proximity of :CCl₂ to phenoxide O).
  • ⭐ Abnormal Reimer–Tiemann: Both ortho positions BLOCKED (e.g. 2,6-dimethylphenol) → :CCl₂ cannot attack ortho → attacks C-1 (ipso, bearing O⁻) → intermediate rearranges → 2,2-dichlorocyclohexadienone derivative. NO –CHO formed. Completely different product!
  • ⭐ Only activated rings react: Phenol ✓, resorcinol ✓ (two –OH → very reactive → CHO at C-2 and C-4 possible), β-naphthol ✓ (→ 2-hydroxy-1-naphthaldehyde)
  • Does NOT work: Benzene ✗, aniline (different reaction), nitrobenzene ✗ (deactivated)
  • Reimer-Tiemann vs Kolbe-Schmitt: R-T → :CCl₂ → –CHO (salicylaldehyde). Kolbe → CO₂ → –COOH (salicylate). Products differ by one oxidation level.
  • Ortho selectivity reason: :CCl₂ is guided to ortho by coordination with adjacent phenoxide oxygen (proximity/intramolecular effect)
⚠
JEE Trap: Abnormal R-T does NOT give salicylaldehyde. When both ortho positions are blocked → 2,2-dichlorocyclohexadienone (no –CHO). If question shows 2,6-disubstituted phenol → expect abnormal product.
💡Remember: The reactive intermediate is :CCl₂ (dichlorocarbene), generated from CHCl₃ by NaOH. :CCl₂ is electrophilic. It is NOT CHCl₃ itself that reacts with the ring.
I — Pinacol–Pinacolone Rearrangement
Core Reaction, Mechanism & Migratory Aptitude ★★★★★
(CH₃)₂C(OH)–C(OH)(CH₃)₂ + H⁺ → (CH₃)₃C–CO–CH₃ (pinacolone) + H₂O
// pinacol (2,3-dimethyl-2,3-butanediol) → pinacolone (3,3-dimethylbutan-2-one)
Mechanism — 4 Steps
1
Protonation: H⁺ protonates one –OH of the 1,2-diol → –OH₂⁺. The OH that gives the MORE STABLE carbocation after leaving is protonated first.
2
Ionization: –OH₂⁺ departs → carbocation (R₂C⁺–C(OH)R₂) formed. More substituted/stable cation preferred.
3
1,2-Migration (RDS): The group with highest migratory aptitude (R = alkyl, aryl, H) on the carbon bearing –OH migrates with its bonding electrons to the adjacent C⁺ → new oxocarbenium ion. STRICTLY INTRAMOLECULAR.
4
Deprotonation: Loss of H⁺ from –OH → ketone (pinacolone) formed as final product
  • ⭐ Migratory aptitude: Aryl > Vinyl > tert-Alkyl > sec-Alkyl > H > Methyl > n-Alkyl
    Mnemonic: "All Very Tired Students Have Massive Notes"
  • ⭐ Product is always a carbonyl compound — ketone (usually) or aldehyde (if H migrates)
  • Driving force: Formation of more stable carbocation + thermodynamically stable C=O product
No Crossover · Semi-Pinacol · Cyclohexane Ring · Key JEE Points
  • ⭐ NO crossover products: Migration is STRICTLY INTRAMOLECULAR — the group NEVER leaves the molecule. Crossover experiments (mix two differently labeled diols) → ZERO exchange of groups between molecules. Proves 1,2-shift is concerted with ionization (intimate ion pair mechanism).
  • ⭐ Semi-Pinacol rearrangement: Only ONE –OH present; adjacent carbon has another leaving group (halide, tosylate) or is part of strained ring → still gives 1,2-shift → ketone/aldehyde. Used for ring expansion or contraction. E.g.: cyclopentane ring with –OH + adjacent –X → semi-pinacol → cyclohexanone (ring expansion).
  • ⭐ Pinacol in cyclohexane ring (trans-cyclohexane-1,2-diol): H⁺ protonates one OH → leaves → carbocation → the anti-periplanar C–C bond of the ring migrates to the cation → cyclohexanone. cis vs trans diol gives DIFFERENT products due to anti-periplanar requirement for migration (draw Newman projection!).
  • ⭐ Unsymmetrical diol selectivity: The OH that generates the more stable carbocation leaves first. Then the group with highest migratory aptitude on the adjacent C (bearing –OH) migrates. Aryl always migrates preferentially if present.
  • H migration: If only H can migrate (no alkyl/aryl on adjacent C) → H migrates → gives aldehyde instead of ketone
  • α-Hydroxy acid connection (JEE Advanced): α-Hydroxy acids (e.g. mandelic acid PhCH(OH)COOH) can undergo analogous acid-catalyzed 1,2-shift with decarboxylation → related to pinacol-type rearrangement
⚠
JEE Traps: (1) Migratory order — H comes AFTER tert-alkyl and sec-alkyl, NOT before aryl. (2) No crossover = intramolecular ALWAYS — never intermolecular. (3) cis-diol and trans-diol of cyclic compounds give DIFFERENT products due to anti-periplanar requirement.
